# My 90-Day DevOps Learning Plan

**Start Date:** May 16, 2026
**Current Level:** BCom 2nd year student — non-IT background, learning DevOps from scratch

---

## What DevOps means to me

DevOps is a culture that brings teams together to build, test, and deploy high-quality software faster. It breaks down silos so that developers, operations managers, QAs, and testers can all communicate effectively instead of working in separate bubbles.

What I really like about it is the continuous integration and delivery side — the pipelines, the automation, the fact that you're involved at every stage. You design and build the whole thing from scratch, and then boom — the software is deployed. That feeling of owning the entire journey from staging to production is what drew me in.

---

## Why I'm learning DevOps & Cloud

I'm a commerce student — nobody around me is doing this, and that's exactly why I want to. I have always been fascinated by technology, and when I explored different career paths, DevOps immediately stood out. It's not just writing code and moving on. You're involved at every stage. You build the pipeline, you own the delivery, you see the whole picture.

Coming from a non-IT background means I have to work harder to get here. But it also means that if I pull this off, it'll mean a lot more. And honestly — it pays well too. I'm not going to pretend that's not part of it.

## Core skills I'm building

- **Kubernetes** — learn it deeply enough to troubleshoot real issues, not just follow docs
- **Networking** — understand it locally and at cloud level, because most problems trace back here
- **Dockerfile writing** — get really good at writing clean, efficient Dockerfiles, not just copying them

## My time budget

3 hours every single day — no exceptions.

That's 21 hours a week. I'm still in college, so some days will be tight. But 3 hours is the floor, not the average.

---

## How I'll stay consistent

Coming from BCom, I don't have the luxury of a CS degree to fall back on. Everything I learn here has to stick because I actually worked for it. So:

- Show up every day — even on exam weeks, even when it's just one concept
- Commit something to GitHub daily — proof that I actually worked
- Write down what I learned in plain words, like I'm explaining it to someone else
- Use the community — ask questions, help others, don't disappear into silent struggle

Nobody handed me a technical background. That's fine. I'll build one.

- **Weeks 1–3:** Linux, Git, shell scripting, networking fundamentals

- **Weeks 8–9:** AWS core services, cloud networking, Terraform basics
